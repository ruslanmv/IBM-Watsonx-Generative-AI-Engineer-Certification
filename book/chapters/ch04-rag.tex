\chapter{Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)}
\label{ch:rag}

\epigraph{``If the facts change weekly, you need retrieval --- not retraining.''}{}

Enterprise knowledge changes faster than models can be retrained. Policies are revised, product terms expire, regulatory language shifts, and internal procedures move from one document system to another. A model that answered correctly last month can be wrong today, even with a perfect prompt. In a regulated business the compliance office typically pushes new policy versions weekly, sometimes faster --- by lunchtime on the day of an update, anything tuned against the previous version is already partially wrong, and a non-grounded assistant cheerfully cites yesterday's table as today's truth.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation exists for this reason. \emph{When the facts move, retrieve them at runtime.} That single rule is the spine of an entire architectural family, and it is the right answer whenever the knowledge in the system is more current than the knowledge inside the model. Section~4 of the exam is RAG, seventeen percent of the test. This chapter walks the pipeline end-to-end --- what an embedding is, how to pick one on watsonx, where to put the vectors, how to retrieve, how to re-rank, how to wire the whole thing in LangChain or LlamaIndex, and where to apply the few quality levers that actually move the needle. By the last page you will have everything a governed Q\&A feature needs to ship against a corpus that updates faster than the model.

\section{Embeddings, in one paragraph (Objective 4.1)}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{assets/figures/fig15-rag-pipeline}
\caption{The full RAG pipeline. Indexing happens offline; retrieval and generation happen online for every query.}
\label{fig:rag-pipeline}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{assets/figures/fig16-embedding-space}
\caption{Embeddings cluster semantically similar text. A query vector lands inside the cluster it is most about, regardless of exact-word overlap.}
\label{fig:embedding-space}
\end{figure}

An \term{embedding} is a vector of floats --- usually between 384 and 1024 dimensions --- that represents the \emph{meaning} of a piece of text. Texts with similar meaning end up near each other in that space, measured by cosine similarity or dot product. A retrieval system embeds every chunk of your corpus once, stores the vectors, and at query time embeds the user's question and returns the nearest chunks. The generator (the LLM) then answers using those chunks as its context. That single round-trip --- embed-query, find-nearest, generate-with-context --- is RAG.

The reason embeddings work in practice is that they catch what keyword search misses. A typical enterprise policy corpus describes the same concept under several different headings --- \emph{loan defaults} in one section, \emph{delinquent borrowers} in another, \emph{non-performing exposures} in a third. A keyword search for \emph{``loan default''} hits only the first; an embedding search hits all three, because the meaning sits close in vector space regardless of vocabulary. That gap is why a watsonx Discovery index using slate-v2 consistently outperforms a pure BM25 search, and why the platform team usually does not waste effort tuning keyword analysers to compensate for missing semantic search.

\subsection{The IBM embedding models worth knowing}

watsonx ships four embedding models that cover almost every realistic enterprise case. The first decision is English-only versus multilingual; the second is small-and-fast versus larger-and-more-accurate.

\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{l c X}

\textbf{Model ID} & \textbf{Dim} & \textbf{Use case} \\
\midrule

Outside IBM there is one open-source model worth knowing by name: \texttt{intfloat/multilingual-e5-large} at 1024 dimensions, which is very strong on cross-lingual retrieval. \texttt{sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2} is the laptop POC default at 384 dims. The BGE family (\texttt{BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5}, \texttt{bge-m3}) is the open-source competitor that shows up in benchmarks. Two properties matter when picking between them all: dimensionality (higher means more recall and more storage), and whether the model is asymmetric --- a retrieval-tuned model uses different encoders for queries and passages, and the \texttt{-rtrvr} naming is your signal.

\examtip{IBM-native, governed, English enterprise RAG points at \textbf{slate-125m-english-rtrvr-v2}. Multilingual corpora point at \textbf{granite-embedding-278m-multilingual}, with \texttt{multilingual-e5-large} as the strongest open alternative.}

\section{Generating embeddings on watsonx (Objective 4.2)}

To call the embeddings API you need three things: an IBM Cloud API key with at least the \emph{Reader} role on the watsonx.ai service, a project ID or space ID to scope and bill the call, and (only if you are calling a deployed custom embedding asset rather than the foundation-model catalogue) a deployment ID. From there, the SDK call is six lines.

\begin{examplebox}[title=\textbf{Get embeddings via the watsonx Python SDK}]
\begin{lstlisting}[style=pythonstyle]
from ibm_watsonx_ai import APIClient, Credentials
from ibm_watsonx_ai.foundation_models import Embeddings

creds  = Credentials(url="https://us-south.ml.cloud.ibm.com",
                     api_key=os.environ["WATSONX_APIKEY"])
client = APIClient(creds, project_id=PROJECT_ID)

emb = Embeddings(model_id="ibm/slate-125m-english-rtrvr-v2",
                 api_client=client)

vectors = emb.embed_documents(texts=[
    "Customer cancelled the contract because of latency.",
    "Refund issued on 2026-03-14 for SKU A-882.",
])
print(len(vectors), len(vectors[0]))   # 2 vectors, 768 dims each
\end{lstlisting}
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Where to put the vectors}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{assets/figures/fig18-vector-db-comparison}
\caption{Vector databases on two axes --- scale (x) and ease of use (y). Watsonx Discovery is the IBM-default for governed enterprise RAG.}
\label{fig:vector-dbs}
\end{figure}

A vector store indexes embeddings so nearest-neighbour search runs in roughly logarithmic rather than linear time. The landscape splits into three camps. The first is \emph{purpose-built vector databases}: \term{FAISS} is the in-memory library you reach for on a laptop or for a single-node corpus; \term{Chroma} is the embedded, persistent option you ship for prototypes and notebooks; \term{Milvus}, \term{Weaviate}, \term{Pinecone}, and \term{Qdrant} are the production-grade distributed and managed stores. The second is \emph{vector extensions to existing databases}: PostgreSQL with \texttt{pgvector} adds a vector type and an HNSW index; Elasticsearch and OpenSearch add a \texttt{dense\_vector} field and support hybrid search in the same index as their classic BM25; Cassandra, SingleStore, and Redis all have first-class vector indexing now. The third is \emph{IBM-native}: \product{watsonx.data} runs managed Milvus collections under governance, and IBM Cloud Databases for Elasticsearch is the IBM-managed hybrid store for enterprise search.

Teams typically pick by where they are in the project. FAISS is the answer when you have to demo by Friday. Chroma is the answer when the demo turns into a small persistent application. Milvus or watsonx Discovery is the answer when the small application becomes a real product with a quarterly index refresh and a regulator who wants every retrieval logged. A common trajectory inside a governed program is six weeks on Chroma for the prototype, then a migration to watsonx Discovery the moment the compliance office asks for audit trails on every retrieval.

\section{Picking the right retriever (Objective 4.3)}

A \term{retriever} is the component that takes a query and returns the most relevant chunks. Vector retrievers --- the kind we just covered --- are one family. The exam also names three other retriever flavours you should recognise on sight.

\product{Watson Discovery} is IBM's mature managed search service. It understands documents (tables, lists, headings), runs NLU enrichments, and ships pre-built retrievers. On the exam it appears as the legacy, fully-managed alternative to a self-built RAG pipeline --- useful in modernisation scenarios where the customer already has Discovery deployed.

The \emph{GitHub Code Retrieval API} is a retrieval-as-a-service endpoint optimised for source code: it understands repo structure, file boundaries, language syntax, and returns code spans that match a natural-language query. When the exam asks about retrieval for code-assistance use cases, this is the right answer, not generic text retrieval.

\product{watsonx Discovery} is the newer, watsonx-aligned discovery service. Managed Elasticsearch under the hood, native hybrid search (BM25 plus vector), tight integration with watsonx.ai prompt templates and the \texttt{ibm-watsonx-ai} SDK, and governance hooks built in. This is the IBM-recommended retriever for new builds, and the right answer whenever a scenario implies IBM-native, scalable, governed, hybrid search.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{X X}
\toprule
\textbf{Scenario} & \textbf{Recommended retriever} \\
\midrule
Prototype on a laptop, $<$100k chunks       & FAISS or Chroma (embedded) \\
Production, single team, $<$10M chunks      & Milvus (managed) or watsonx Discovery \\
Production, multi-tenant, governed           & \textbf{watsonx Discovery} (Elasticsearch + governance) \\
Source-code Q\&A across repos                & GitHub Code Retrieval API + LLM \\

\end{tabularx}

\end{table}

The \textbf{GitHub Code Retrieval API} is for source code. Read the model identifier carefully.}

\section{Implementing RAG: four working patterns (Objective 4.4)}

The canonical pipeline runs Load $\to$ Chunk $\to$ Embed $\to$ Index $\to$ Retrieve $\to$ (Re-rank) $\to$ Augment prompt $\to$ Generate. Every pattern below is a different combination of which library handles which step.

\subsection{Chunking, the step that dominates quality}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{assets/figures/fig17-chunking-strategies}
\caption{Three common chunking strategies. Start with recursive; move to semantic only if retrieval quality is poor.}
\label{fig:chunking}
\end{figure}

How you slice a document drives retrieval quality more than which vector store you pick. The strategies worth knowing are short to list. \emph{Fixed-size} splits every $N$ characters or tokens with $K$ overlap --- simplest, breaks meaning at boundaries. \emph{Recursive character} splits on paragraph breaks first, then line breaks, then sentence boundaries --- LangChain's default and a good general balance. \emph{Semantic} splits embed sentences and cluster by similarity --- the best quality but the most compute. \emph{Document-aware} uses Markdown headings, HTML tags, or code blocks --- the right choice for structured corpora. \emph{Sliding window} adds 10--20\% overlap to any of the above to reduce boundary loss.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{l X X}
\toprule
\textbf{Strategy} & \textbf{How it works} & \textbf{Pros / cons} \\
\midrule
Fixed-size         & $N$ chars/tokens with $K$ overlap               & Simple; breaks meaning at boundaries \\
Recursive char     & Split on \texttt{\textbackslash n\textbackslash n}, then \texttt{\textbackslash n}, then sentence & LangChain default; good general balance \\
Semantic           & Embed sentences, cluster by similarity          & Best quality; most compute \\
Document-aware     & Use Markdown headings / HTML tags / code blocks & Best on structured corpora \\
Sliding window     & Overlap by 10--20\% of chunk size               & Reduces boundary loss \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\caption{Chunking strategies.}
\label{tab:chunking}
\end{table}

The rule of thumb most teams converge on: chunks of 256 to 1024 tokens with 10 to 20 percent overlap. Smaller chunks give more precise retrieval but lose surrounding context; larger chunks give more context but make retrieval noisier. A typical governed assistant settles at 512 tokens with 64-token overlap on recursive splits, and only moves to semantic chunking for a single hard-to-retrieve sub-corpus of dense regulatory text.

\subsection{Pattern A --- LangChain plus watsonx LLM, the default}

This is the pattern most teams reach for first. Load PDFs, chunk recursively, embed with slate-125, index in FAISS for development or in watsonx Discovery for production, retrieve with MMR for diversity, generate with Granite. The code below is the entire pipeline for a governed policy-Q\&A feature in development.

\begin{examplebox}[title=\textbf{End-to-end RAG with LangChain and watsonx}]
\begin{lstlisting}[style=pythonstyle]
from langchain_ibm import WatsonxLLM, WatsonxEmbeddings
from langchain_community.vectorstores import FAISS
from langchain_community.document_loaders import PyPDFLoader
from langchain.text_splitter import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
from langchain.chains import RetrievalQA
import glob

# 1) Load
docs = []
for p in glob.glob("./policies/*.pdf"):
    docs.extend(PyPDFLoader(p).load())

# 2) Chunk
splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(chunk_size=512, chunk_overlap=64)
chunks   = splitter.split_documents(docs)

# 3) Embed + index
emb = WatsonxEmbeddings(model_id="ibm/slate-125m-english-rtrvr-v2",
                        url=URL, apikey=API_KEY, project_id=PROJECT_ID)
store = FAISS.from_documents(chunks, emb)

# 4) Generator + retrieval QA chain
llm = WatsonxLLM(model_id="ibm/granite-3-2-8b-instruct",
                 url=URL, apikey=API_KEY, project_id=PROJECT_ID,
                 params={"decoding_method": "greedy",
                         "max_new_tokens": 300, "temperature": 0.2})

qa = RetrievalQA.from_chain_type(
        llm=llm,
        retriever=store.as_retriever(search_type="mmr",
                                     search_kwargs={"k": 4, "fetch_k": 20}),
        return_source_documents=True)

print(qa({"query": "What is the enterprise refund policy?"}))
\end{lstlisting}
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Pattern B --- LangChain plus Elasticsearch, the governed default}

For governed enterprise deployments, swap FAISS for Elasticsearch's \texttt{dense\_vector} index (or watsonx Discovery, which fronts Elasticsearch under the hood). The win is hybrid search --- BM25 plus dense vectors with reciprocal-rank fusion --- in the same index, which on factual queries with rare terms (policy numbers, account IDs) routinely beats pure dense retrieval.

\begin{examplebox}[title=\textbf{Elasticsearch-backed retriever}]
\begin{lstlisting}[style=pythonstyle]
from langchain_elasticsearch import ElasticsearchStore

store = ElasticsearchStore.from_documents(
    chunks,
    embedding=emb,
    es_url=os.environ["ES_URL"],
    es_user="elastic",
    es_password=os.environ["ES_PASS"],
    index_name="policy_rag")

# Hybrid search (BM25 + dense vector + RRF fusion)
retriever = store.as_retriever(
    search_type="similarity",
    search_kwargs={"k": 4, "strategy": "hybrid"})
\end{lstlisting}
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Pattern C --- LangChain plus SingleStore, the one-store option}

SingleStore offers vector indexes inside a transactional and analytical database. The architectural appeal is that you keep one store for vectors and structured records together --- handy when your retrieval has to join chunks against customer or transaction data the LLM should not see.

\begin{examplebox}[title=\textbf{SingleStore-backed retriever}]
\begin{lstlisting}[style=pythonstyle]
from langchain_community.vectorstores import SingleStoreDB

store = SingleStoreDB.from_documents(chunks, embedding=emb,
                                     host=DB_URL, database="rag",
                                     table_name="policy_chunks")
retriever = store.as_retriever(search_kwargs={"k": 4})
\end{lstlisting}
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Pattern D --- LlamaIndex, the RAG-opinionated alternative}

\product{LlamaIndex} is an alternative to LangChain that ships with first-class RAG concepts --- \emph{Index}, \emph{Node}, \emph{Retriever}, \emph{Response Synthesiser}. It is more opinionated than LangChain about how a RAG pipeline should look, which makes it faster to ship a default pipeline and harder to escape the defaults when you outgrow them. Teams I've worked with split roughly evenly between the two; the patterns are inter-convertible.

\begin{examplebox}[title=\textbf{LlamaIndex + watsonx}]
\begin{lstlisting}[style=pythonstyle]
from llama_index.core import VectorStoreIndex, SimpleDirectoryReader, Settings
from llama_index.llms.ibm import WatsonxLLM as WatsonxLLM_LI
from llama_index.embeddings.ibm import WatsonxEmbeddings as WatsonxEmb_LI

Settings.llm        = WatsonxLLM_LI(model_id="ibm/granite-3-2-8b-instruct", ...)
Settings.embed_model = WatsonxEmb_LI(model_id="ibm/slate-125m-english-rtrvr-v2", ...)

docs  = SimpleDirectoryReader("./policies").load_data()
index = VectorStoreIndex.from_documents(docs)
print(index.as_query_engine().query("What is the enterprise refund policy?"))
\end{lstlisting}
\end{examplebox}

\subsection{Re-ranking, the single biggest quality lever}

A \term{cross-encoder re-ranker} takes the top $N$ candidates from the retriever and re-scores each one against the actual query --- more accurate than embedding similarity alone, typically a 10--20 percent boost on recall at $k$. The reason it works: an embedding similarity is symmetric and per-vector, while a cross-encoder reads the query and the passage together and produces one score for the pair. watsonx.ai exposes a re-rank endpoint at \texttt{POST /ml/v1/text/rerank}; the default model in 2026 is \texttt{cross-encoder/ms-marco-minilm-l-12-v2} or an IBM equivalent. If you read this whole chapter and only add one thing to your RAG pipeline, add a re-ranker.

\subsection{Agentic RAG --- when one retrieval is not enough}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{assets/figures/fig19-agentic-rag-loop}
\caption{Agentic RAG: the agent decides whether one retrieval was enough or to refine the query and try again. Loops until confident, then answers.}
\label{fig:agentic-rag}
\end{figure}

\term{Agentic RAG} treats retrieval as a tool that the agent decides whether to use, how to call, and whether to call again. Compared to plain RAG, the agent can rewrite the user's query into one or several refined searches, call multiple retrievers (policy DB, code repo, FAQ index), decide \emph{not} to retrieve when the answer is obvious, and verify with a second retrieval pass when the first answer looks weak. The trade-off is the obvious one: better accuracy on complex queries, higher latency and token cost on simple ones. A governed assistant typically uses agentic RAG only on its escalation path, where a single retrieval almost never carries the answer; for plain policy-Q\&A, one retrieval is enough.

\subsection{AutoRAG --- hyperparameter optimisation for the pipeline}

\term{AutoRAG} is a pattern and a small set of open-source tools that automatically tune the RAG pipeline --- choosing the chunk size, the embedder, the retriever's $k$, the re-ranker, and the generator parameters to maximise a target metric on an evaluation set. It is hyperparameter optimisation applied to RAG; if you have a labelled eval set and the patience to run a search, it works. On the exam, recognise the name and the concept.

\section{Where to spend your improvement budget}

If retrieval quality is not good enough, the levers below are worth pulling in this order. I have done this exercise on a dozen RAG projects and the ranking holds.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \emph{Better chunking.} Document-aware splits beat fixed-size on any structured corpus. This is almost always the first win.
    \item \emph{Hybrid search.} BM25 plus dense vectors --- in watsonx Discovery or Elasticsearch --- helps most on queries with rare specific terms.
    \item \emph{Cross-encoder re-ranking.} The single biggest quality jump per hour of engineering.
    \item \emph{Query rewriting.} A small LLM expands ambiguous queries before retrieval.
    \item \emph{Better embedder.} Slate-125 over Slate-30, or the multilingual Granite at 278M.
    \item \emph{Larger or smarter generator.} Last resort, expensive, often unnecessary if the retrieval is good.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Production readiness in one paragraph}

A RAG pipeline that is ready for production carries metadata on every chunk (source, page, last-updated timestamp, ACL), returns citations the prompt instructs the model to use, has a system prompt that says \emph{``answer only from the context, and if absent say so''}, runs Granite Guardian's groundedness check on the output, has a re-embedding job wired to document updates (watsonx Pipelines or a scheduled job), evaluates against a held-out set of fifty to a hundred ground-truth Q\&A pairs on every release with Recall@$k$ and answer faithfulness as the metrics, and pipes lineage and metrics into watsonx.governance. Anything less than that and you are calling your prototype a product.

\begin{takeawaybox}
RAG is a chain of decisions and the exam tests them out of order. The chain looks the same on every project: pick an embedder (English slate or multilingual Granite for IBM-native answers), put the vectors somewhere (Chroma for prototypes, watsonx Discovery for production), chunk the documents in a way that respects their structure, retrieve with a healthy $k$, re-rank with a cross-encoder for the single biggest quality jump, prompt the generator to ground in the retrieved context and cite, then wrap the output with Granite Guardian's groundedness check. When the basic chain stops being enough --- multi-hop queries, ambiguous user phrasing --- step up to agentic RAG, where retrieval becomes a tool the agent decides to use. When the parameters of the chain themselves need tuning, that is AutoRAG.
\end{takeawaybox}
