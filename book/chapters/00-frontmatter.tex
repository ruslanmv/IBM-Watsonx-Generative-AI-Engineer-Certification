\chapter*{Preface}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Preface}

\epigraph{``The half-life of a tool in this field has become shorter than the half-life of a project that uses it.''}{}

\noindent The last three years have rewritten the playbook for every IT organisation that ships software. What used to be a research field --- large language models, retrieval-augmented generation, agentic systems, vector databases, governed model serving --- is now production infrastructure, and the technologies underneath it are turning over every quarter. Model catalogues that were authoritative twelve months ago are now half-deprecated. Best practices for prompt design, fine-tuning, evaluation, and safety filtering are settling, but only just. Vendors are racing to publish updates that engineers have to digest at the same speed.

This book exists because the certification at its centre --- the \textbf{IBM watsonx Generative AI Engineer Associate}, exam code \textbf{C1000-185} --- happens to be one of the clearest, vendor-published checklists of what an engineer needs to know to keep pace with that pipeline. It does not capture everything, and no single certification can. But its six objectives --- analysis and design, prompt engineering, fine-tuning, retrieval-augmented generation, deployment, and integration --- map almost exactly onto the topics that a modern IT team needs to staff in \the\year{}. Preparing for the exam is, in practice, preparing yourself for the next twenty months of work.

\section*{Why this guide exists, and why now}

Three things have changed in a way that justifies a study guide rather than a blog post.

The first is the \emph{cadence of the model catalogue}. The watsonx.ai catalogue rotated several times in the eighteen months before this edition went to press. \texttt{granite-13b-instruct-v2}, \texttt{llama-2-70b-chat}, \texttt{flan-t5-xxl}, and the \texttt{mpt-*} family have all been deprecated. Granite~3.x, Llama~3.x, Mistral, Granite Guardian, Granite Embedding and Slate~v2 took their place. Every code listing, every model identifier, and every API parameter in this book has been validated against the current \texttt{ibm-watsonx-ai} Python SDK and the public watsonx.ai REST API. Where a deprecated identifier appears, it is flagged so candidates can recognise it on the exam without picking it in new work.

The second is the \emph{shape of the work itself}. Three years ago, an AI engineer's day was building models. Today it is selecting between off-the-shelf foundation models, designing the prompt and retrieval layers that make them useful, customising them with parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA and InstructLab, and shipping all of that into governed production systems. The exam reflects that shift: roughly one-third of the questions are about fine-tuning, and most of the rest are about the prompt, retrieval, deployment, and integration layers that wrap a foundation model. There is almost nothing in this book about training a transformer from scratch, because almost nobody on a production team does that.

The third is the \emph{maturity of safety and governance}. Generative AI's first wave shipped without much of either. The second wave --- the one this book documents --- ships them by default. Granite Guardian, watsonx.governance factsheets, drift monitoring, demographic-parity tests, audit-grade logging --- they are no longer optional features. The certification tests them as core engineering skills, and so does this book.

\section*{The AI boom, in three numbers}

Three numbers carry the context. Spending on generative AI infrastructure has moved from a rounding error in 2022 to a major line in nearly every enterprise IT budget in \the\year{}. The number of public foundation models worth considering in production has gone from a handful to dozens, with model families turning over roughly every six to twelve months. And the volume of regulation around how those models can be deployed has moved from almost zero to a working framework in every major jurisdiction. An engineer who is current on the technology stack but absent on the governance layer is now half-trained; the same is true in the other direction. This book treats both as part of the same job.

\section*{The Acme case study --- one thread through every chapter}

To keep the material grounded, the book follows a single fictional engagement from beginning to end. \textbf{Acme Bank} --- the canonical placeholder for any large, regulated enterprise --- has asked its AI team to build the \textbf{Acme Assistant}, an internal tool for the bank's customer-support staff. The Assistant must answer policy questions, draft replies to customer emails, summarise long claim threads, and escalate cleanly when it should not answer at all. Cost matters. Compliance matters. Audit trails matter.

The AI engineer at Acme is, in the prose that follows, sometimes \emph{they}, sometimes \emph{you}, sometimes \emph{the team}. The case study is the thread that ties Chapter~1 (\emph{what is the Assistant actually supposed to do?}), Chapter~2 (\emph{the first prompt and what broke}), Chapter~3 (\emph{when prompting alone wasn't enough}), Chapter~4 (\emph{the policy documents change every Monday}), Chapter~5 (\emph{rolling it out to eight hundred agents}), and Chapter~6 (\emph{wiring it to Watson Assistant and the bank's CRM}). You can read each chapter on its own --- the book is also a reference --- but the Acme thread is what turns the abstract decisions into decisions you can argue with.

\section*{Who this book is for}

This is a book for the AI engineer, data scientist, or solution architect who needs to operate confidently on a watsonx platform and pass the C1000-185 exam on the way there. If you have shipped a Python service before, written a SQL query under deadline, and read a vendor whitepaper without losing your mind, you have everything you need. You do not need a deep-learning background, although a basic familiarity with machine-learning vocabulary will accelerate you through the first chapter. You do need patience for the moments when the book asks you to memorise something that looks arbitrary --- those are almost always the moments the exam tests.

\section*{How this book is organised}

The book has three layers. The \emph{chapters} explain --- one chapter per official exam section, weighted to match the blueprint. The \emph{appendices} are reference and practice --- a glossary, two full mock exams with answer keys, a one-page cheatsheet, and an objective-by-objective map back into the chapters. The \emph{index} at the back lets you find a single term, model identifier, or watsonx product without scanning the table of contents.

Inside a chapter, the rhythm is intentionally varied. Some sections open with a problem from the Acme engagement and arrive at the concept through the problem. Some open with the concept and immediately put it to work. A short \emph{exam tip} appears where the certification has a recognisable question pattern; a \emph{warning} appears where a deprecation or a common pitfall could cost points. There is no fixed template. If you read the same kind of box on every page you would stop noticing them, and the book would stop working.

\subsection*{Workbook components}

The chapter-end practice questions are the first feedback loop --- short, scenario-based, with answer keys at the end of each chapter. The two mock exams (Appendices~B and~E, with answer keys in C and F) are the second loop: take them under timed conditions with a phone in another room. Appendix~A is the glossary. Appendix~D is the one-page cheatsheet candidates should be able to reproduce from memory by exam day. Appendix~G is the objective-to-page map --- after a mock, tally the objectives you missed and re-read just those pages.

\section*{The exam, in five numbers}

The certification is officially titled \emph{IBM Certified watsonx Generative AI Engineer --- Associate}, exam code \textbf{C1000-185}. It is delivered by Pearson VUE, either at a test centre or online with a proctor, and IBM expects roughly six to twelve months of hands-on watsonx.ai experience before a candidate sits it. The numbers worth memorising fit on one hand: \textbf{sixty-two questions}, \textbf{ninety minutes}, \textbf{forty-four to pass} (about seventy-one percent), \textbf{six sections}, and roughly \textbf{eighty-seven seconds per question} on average. Pacing matters more than raw recall.

\subsection*{Where the points live}

The six exam sections are not equally weighted. Figure~\ref{fig:blueprint} shows them stacked by their share of the test, with the chapter in this book that covers each. Section~3 --- fine-tuning --- is thirty-one percent of the exam, almost a third. That is by design: parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), LoRA, quantisation, and IBM's InstructLab are the topics IBM most wants candidates to demonstrate, and the chapter ratio in this book is built around that reality.

Fine-tuning is the largest single section, and the section most engineers find least familiar coming in. That gap is exactly where points are won and lost.}

A month is the right amount of time. Less, and you cram; more, and the first chapters fade before exam day. The plan below front-loads fine-tuning (which earns its 31\% of the exam), uses the back half of week three for the integration material that engineers tend to under-study, and reserves the final week for timed mocks and the cheatsheet. The exact day-count is less important than the rhythm: every week ends with practice questions, not with new material.

\section*{What you should bring to the first page}

This is not a beginner's introduction to machine learning. A working comfort with Python --- enough to read a script, run a notebook, and call a REST API --- is the floor. Some prior exposure to cloud platforms (preferably IBM Cloud, but any major cloud will do) makes the deployment chapter easier. A watsonx.ai account is required for the labs in the companion repository; the free trial covers everything used in this book.

If you have never seen a transformer block, never trained a model, and never written a prompt longer than two sentences, that is fine. The first chapter assumes none of that. What you cannot fake is the rhythm of working code. Read every code listing slowly the first time; the second time, copy it into a notebook and run it.

\section*{Conventions used in this book}

Three typographic rules run through every chapter. \texttt{Monospaced text} is code, file paths, API parameters, and model identifiers (\texttt{ibm/granite-3-2-8b-instruct} is one). \emph{Italics} introduce a new term where it is first defined, and occasionally mark an aside. \textbf{Bold} marks the few words a candidate should be able to recite back from memory.

Coloured boxes appear sparingly. A \textcolor{ibmsuccess}{\textbf{Key Concept}} states a rule worth memorising; an \textcolor{ibmblue}{\textbf{Example}} gives a concrete scenario, usually from the Acme engagement; an \textcolor{ibmwarning}{\textbf{Important}} or \textcolor{ibmwarning}{\textbf{Exam Tip}} flags a question pattern the certification uses; a \textcolor{ibmerror}{\textbf{Warning}} marks a pitfall or a deprecated identifier; a \textcolor{ibmgray}{\textbf{Practice Question}} is a self-test with an answer at the end of the chapter. If a chapter ever feels like every paragraph is in a box, that is a bug.
